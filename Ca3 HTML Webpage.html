The Importance of Strong Passwords and Multi-Factor Authentication

What is a password and why is it necessary to protect your personal data?

Passwords are a secret word, phrase, or character that you create to prove who you are so you can access your accounts. They protect your personal information and online accounts from being accessed by others. Without a password, anyone could access your messages, photos, bank accounts, or personal files.

The importance of creating strong passwords

A strong password acts as the first line of defense against unauthorized access to your personal and professional accounts. With cyberattacks becoming more sophisticated, choosing a password that is easy to remember yet hard for others to guess is crucial. A strong password is characterized by its length, complexity, and uniqueness. Ideally, this complexity makes it exponentially harder for attackers to crack through brute force attacks. However, the strength of a password is not solely determined by its complexity. Uniqueness is equally important. Reusing passwords across multiple accounts is a common practice, yet it poses a significant risk. If one account is compromised, all other accounts using the same password are at risk. Therefore, creating a unique, strong password for each account is imperative. There are also some passwords manager tools you can install and use to store all your different passwords If you are afraid of losing them.

What is a strong password?

  1. At least 12 characters long.
  2. Include a mix of upper and lower case letters, numbers and symbols.
  3. Create new passwords for different accounts (do not reuse passwords).
  4. Use a password manager.

Some examples of password manager tools:

  • LastPass
  • Bitwarden
  • 1Password
  • Dashlane

Why Multi-factor authentication?

With the rise of sophisticated hacking techniques, passwords alone can no longer protect against unauthorized access and security attacks. That was true even before the pandemic, when the user access-related security threats had grown commonplace. In the new normal, the number of successful attacks is staggering.

Factors used in Multi-Factor Authentication:

Something You Know
A password, PIN, or answer to a security question.
Something You Have
A device like a phone or hardware token that receives a one-time code.
Something You Are
Biometric data such as a fingerprint, facial recognition, or retina scan.

Comparison of Authentication Factors:

Factor Type Example
Knowledge (Something you know) Password, PIN
Possession (Something You Have) Phone, Authenticator app, Security key
Inherence (Something You Are) Fingerprint, Face ID

Forms of Multi-Factor Authentication

  1. SMS code or Email OTP – a one-time code sent to your phone or email.
  2. Authenticator apps – apps that generate time-based codes (e.g. Google Authenticator).
  3. Smartphone push notification – like “Approve sign-in?” prompts.
